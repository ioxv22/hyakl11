Conditional Sentences
First Conditional  &  Second Conditional
Grade 12  |  English Language
First Conditional  —  اﻟﻘﺎﻋدة اﻷوﻟﻰ
Structure:
      If  +  Present Simple  ,  will  +  base verb
When do we use it?
We use the First Conditional to talk about real and possible situations in the future. The condition is
likely to happen.
Examples:
  
✓
  If it rains tomorrow, we will cancel the picnic.
  
✓
  If you study hard, you will pass the exam.
  
✓
  If she arrives late, the teacher will not let her in.
  
✓
  If they finish early, they will go to the park.
Important Notes:
  •  Never use 'will' in the If-clause (the condition part).
  •  Use a comma after the If-clause when it comes first.
  •  The If-clause can come second — no comma is needed in that case.
  ✗  Wrong:   If it will rain, we will stay home.
  
✓
  Correct: If it rains, we will stay home.
  Both of these are correct:
  
✓
  If you are tired, you will make mistakes.
  
✓
  You will make mistakes if you are tired.
Second Conditional  —  اﻟﻘﺎﻋدة اﻟﺛﺎﻧﯾﺔ
Structure:
      If  +  Past Simple  ,  would  +  base verb
When do we use it?
We use the Second Conditional to talk about unreal, imaginary, or very unlikely situations in the present
or future. The speaker knows the situation is not true.
Examples:
  
✓
  If I had a million dollars, I would buy a big house.
  
✓
  If she were a doctor, she would help a lot of people.
  
✓
  If we lived near the beach, we would swim every day.
  
✓
  If he spoke English fluently, he would apply for that job.
Important Notes:
  •  Use 'were' for ALL subjects (I / he / she / it) in formal writing, not 'was'.
  •  Never use 'would' in the If-clause.
  •  'Could' or 'might' can replace 'would' to show less certainty.
  ✗  Wrong:   If I would have a car, I would drive to work.
  
✓
  Correct: If I had a car, I would drive to work.
  Formal vs Informal:
  
✓
  Formal:   If I were the manager, I would change the rules.
  
✓
  Informal: If I was the manager, I would change the rules.
Rasha Aljameel
Key Differences at a Glance
First Conditional Second Conditional
Form If + Present Simple,  will + verb If + Past Simple,  would + verb
Meaning Real / possible situation in the future Unreal / imaginary situation (now or
future)
Probability Likely to happen Unlikely or impossible
Time Present / Future Present / Future (but imaginary)
Example If it rains, I will stay home. If I had a car, I would drive to work.
Note The situation could really happen. The situation is just imagination.
More Comparison Examples:
  First:  If I find my keys, I will leave now.   →  (I might find them)
  Second: If I found my keys, I would leave now.  →  (I can't find them — imaginary)
  First:  If she studies, she will get a good grade.   →  (possible)
  Second: If she studied, she would get a good grade.  →  (she probably won't study)

Lesson 1 — G.11.1: Non-Separable Phrasal Verbs
1. What is a Phrasal Verb?
A phrasal verb is a combination of a verb + a particle (a preposition or an adverb) that together create a
meaning different from the original verb.
Structure: VERB + PARTICLE = new meaning
• look + after = take care of
• run + into = meet by chance
2. The Rule of Non-Separable Phrasal Verbs
A non-separable phrasal verb is one whose verb and particle CANNOT be split. The object — whether
a noun or a pronoun — must always come AFTER the particle
.
Pattern:
✔ Subject + Verb + Particle + Object
✘ Subject + Verb + Object + Particle  (WRONG)
3. Examples
• She looks after her little brother every weekend.  — (correct)
• She looks her little brother after.  — (incorrect — cannot be separated)
• I ran into an old friend at the airport.  — (correct)
• We rely on our teammates for support.  — (correct)
• The thieves got away with the money.  — (correct)

Lesson 2 — G.11.2: Separable Phrasal Verbs
1. The Rule of Separable Phrasal Verbs
A separable phrasal verb is one whose verb and particle CAN be split by the object.
Two important sub-rules:
If the object is a NOUN: it can come either between the verb and the particle, or after the particle.

With a NOUN object — both correct:

With a PRONOUN object — only one correct:
• Please turn it off.  — (correct)

Lesson 1 — Present Modals
1. What is a Modal Verb?
A modal verb is a special auxiliary (helping) verb that adds meaning to the main verb — such as ability,
possibility, permission, obligation, or advice.
2. The Golden Rules of Modals
A modal is always followed by the BASE FORM of the verb (no -s, no -ing, no to).
Modals do NOT change with the subject (no 's' for he/she/it).
To make a NEGATIVE: add 'not' after the modal (e.g., cannot, must not, should not).
To make a QUESTION: put the modal BEFORE the subject (e.g., Can you...? Should I...?).
Pattern:  Subject + MODAL + base verb + ...
• She can swim.  — (NOT: She can swims / She can to swim)
• He must leave now.  — (NOT: He musts leave)
Rasha Aljameel

could polite request / possibility Could you help me, please?
may formal permission / possibility You may leave the room now.
might weak possibility It might rain later.
must strong obligation / strong
certainty You must wear a seatbelt.
must not prohibition (forbidden) You must not smoke here.
should / ought to advice / recommendation You should drink more water.
will future / promise / decision I will call you tomorrow.
would polite offer / hypothetical Would you like some tea?
shall offer / suggestion (formal) Shall we begin the meeting?
3. The Main Present Modals and Their Uses
4. Important Distinctions
must vs. mustn't vs. don't have to
must = it is necessary / obligation
must not (mustn't) = it is forbidden / prohibited
don't have to = it is NOT necessary (no obligation, but you can if you want)
• You must wear a uniform.  — (it's required)
• You mustn't park here.  — (forbidden)
• You don't have to come.  — (not required, but you can)
can vs. could vs. may
can = informal ability or permission

Lesson 2 — Semi-Modals
1. What is a Semi-Modal?
Semi-modals (also called marginal modals) are verbs that have a similar meaning to modal verbs but
behave partly like ordinary verbs. Unlike pure modals, semi-modals CAN change form (add -s, -ed, or
use 'do/does/did' for questions and negatives).
2. The Main Semi-Modals
Semi-Modal Function Example
have to / has to obligation (external rule) She has to wear a uniform at school.
don't have to no obligation / not necessary You don't have to pay; it's free.
need to necessity I need to finish this report today.
don't need to no necessity You don't need to bring food.
ought to advice / moral duty (= should) You ought to apologize to her.
be able to ability (= can, used in all tenses) She is able to solve the problem.
be allowed to permission (= may/can) Children are allowed to enter free.
be supposed to expectation / mild obligation You are supposed to wear a tie.
had better strong advice / warning You had better leave now or you'll be late.
used to past habit (no longer true) I used to play football every weekend.

Lesson 3 — Past Simple
1. Use of the Past Simple
The Past Simple is used to talk about completed actions, events, or states that happened at a definite
time in the past.
A finished action at a specific past time:  I visited Paris last summer.
A series of completed past actions:  She woke up, brushed her teeth, and left.
Past habits or repeated actions (often with adverbs of frequency):  We often played football after
school.
Past states:  He was a doctor in 2010.
2. Form — Regular Verbs
Add -ed to the base form of the verb.
work → worked
play → played
watch → watched
Spelling rules:
Verb ending in -e: add only -d  (live → lived, arrive → arrived)
Verb ending in consonant + y: change y → i, then add -ed  (study → studied, try → tried)
Short verb (1 vowel + 1 consonant): double the final consonant + -ed  (stop → stopped, plan →
planned)
Rasha Aljameel
3. Form — Irregular Verbs
Irregular verbs do NOT follow the -ed rule. You must memorize them.
Base Form Past Simple Example
go went She went home early.
eat ate We ate pizza yesterday.
see saw I saw a great film.
buy bought He bought a new phone.
take took They took the early bus.
come came She came to my party.
write wrote He wrote a long letter.
be was / were I was tired. They were happy.
have had We had a great time.
do did She did her homework.
4. Negatives and Questions
For all verbs (except 'be' and modals), use the auxiliary 'did' / 'did not (didn't)'.
Important: After 'did/didn't', use the BASE FORM — NOT the past form.
Negative pattern:  Subject + did not (didn't) + base verb
• She didn't go to school.  — (NOT: She didn't went)
• They didn't watch the film.
Question pattern:  Did + subject + base verb ... ?
• Did you finish your homework?  — (NOT: Did you finished?)
• Where did he go?
With 'be' (was/were), no auxiliary is needed:
• She was not at home.
• Were you tired yesterday?

Lesson 4 — Past Time: 'Used to'
1. Use of 'Used to'
'Used to' is used to talk about:
Past habits or repeated actions that DO NOT happen anymore.
Past states or situations that are NO LONGER true.
The key idea: it WAS true in the past, but it is NOT true now.
• I used to smoke, but I quit last year.  — (I don't smoke now.)
• She used to live in Cairo.  — (She doesn't live there anymore.)
• There used to be a cinema here.  — (There isn't one now.)
2. Form
Affirmative:  Subject + used to + base verb
• He used to play tennis every weekend.
• They used to be best friends.
Negative:  Subject + didn't use to + base verb
⚠
 Note: in the negative, we write 'use to' (without -d), because 'did' already carries the past tense.
• She didn't use to like coffee.  — (NOT: She didn't used to)
• I didn't use to wake up early.
Question:  Did + subject + use to + base verb ... ?
• Did you use to play any sports?
• Where did he use to live?
3. 'Used to' vs. 'Past Simple'
Both can describe past actions, but there is a difference:
'Used to' = repeated action / state in the past, no longer true now.
Past Simple = a single completed action, OR something true at a specific past time.
• I used to live in Dubai.  — (for a long period, but not anymore)
• I lived in Dubai in 2015.  — (specific time, just stating the fact)
4. Common Mistakes to Avoid
✘ He uses to play football. → ✔ He used to play football. (always past form)
✘ She didn't used to study. → ✔ She didn't use to study.
✘ Did you used to live here? → ✔ Did you use to live here?
✘ I used to playing tennis. → ✔ I used to play tennis. (base form, not -ing)
⚠
 Don't confuse 'used to' with 'be used to' / 'get used to':
used to + verb = a past habit (no longer true)
be used to + noun/-ing = be accustomed to (familiar with something)
• I used to drink coffee.  — (I don't anymore.)
• I am used to drinking coffee.  — (I am familiar with it; it's normal for me.)
Rasha Aljameel

Reported Speech
Reporting Verbs & Patterns
Rules • Examples • Multiple-Choice Practice
1. What is Reported Speech?
Reported Speech (also called Indirect Speech) is the way we tell someone what another person said,
without using their exact words.
Compare:
• Direct Speech: Sara said, "I am tired."
• Reported Speech: Sara said (that) she was tired.
In direct speech we use quotation marks for the exact words. In reported speech we put the message
into our own words and usually change the tense and the pronouns.
2. Reporting with 'said' / 'told'
Said vs. Told
said → not followed by an object directly. (She said that... / She said to me that...)
told → MUST be followed by an object (the listener). (She told ME that...)
• He said (that) he was busy.  — (correct)
• He said me that he was busy.  — (WRONG)
• He told me (that) he was busy.  — (correct)
• He told that he was busy.  — (WRONG)
Rasha Aljameel
3. Tense Backshift (The Golden Rule)
When the reporting verb is in the PAST (said, told, asked...), the verb in the original sentence
usually goes ONE STEP BACK in time.
Direct Speech → Reported Speech Example
Present Simple (work) Past Simple (worked) "I work here." → He said he
worked there.
Present Continuous (am
working) Past Continuous (was working) "I am working." → He said he was
working.
Present Perfect (have
worked) Past Perfect (had worked) "I have worked." → He said he
had worked.
Past Simple (worked) Past Perfect (had worked) "I worked." → He said he had
worked.
will → would future "I will go." → She said she would
go.
can → could ability/permission "I can swim." → He said he could
swim.
may → might possibility "I may come." → She said she
might come.
must → had to obligation "I must leave." → He said he had
to leave.
Modals that DO NOT change
These modals stay the same in reported speech: would, could, might, should, ought to, used to.
• "You should rest." → She said I should rest.
Rasha Aljameel
4. Changes in Pronouns, Time and Place
Pronouns
Pronouns change to match the new speaker's perspective.
• Sara: "I love MY brother." → Sara said SHE loved HER brother.
• Tom told me, "I will help YOU." → Tom told me HE would help ME.
Time and Place Expressions
Direct Speech → Reported Speech
now then / at that moment
today that day
tonight that night
yesterday the day before / the previous day
tomorrow the next day / the following day
last week the week before / the previous week
next month the following month
ago before
this / these that / those
here there
• "I saw him YESTERDAY." → She said she had seen him THE DAY BEFORE.
• "I will call you TOMORROW." → He said he would call me THE NEXT DAY.
Rasha Aljameel
5. Reporting Questions
Important rules:
Use 'asked' (or 'wanted to know', 'wondered').
Change the question to NORMAL word order (subject + verb), NOT question word order.
Do NOT use the auxiliary 'do/does/did' in the reported question.
Do NOT use a question mark.
Wh-Questions
Use the same wh-word (what, where, when, why, who, how) as a linker.
• Direct: He asked, "Where do you live?"
• Reported: He asked where I lived.  — (NOT: where did I live)
• Direct: She said, "What time does the train leave?"
• Reported: She asked what time the train left.
Yes/No Questions
Use 'if' or 'whether' as a linker.
• Direct: He asked, "Are you tired?"
• Reported: He asked if I was tired.

Pattern:  Reporting verb + object + (NOT) + to + base verb
Common verbs: tell, ask, order, command, beg, advise, warn, remind.

• Reported: She told me to close the door.
• Direct: "Please help me."
• Reported: He asked me to help him.
• Direct: "Don't touch the wires!"

7. Reporting Verbs and Their Patterns
Instead of always using 'said' or 'told', we can use more specific reporting verbs that show the speaker's
intention. Each verb follows a specific grammatical pattern
.
Pattern 1: verb + to + infinitive
Verbs: agree, decide, offer, promise, refuse, threaten, claim
• "I will help you." → He promised to help me.
• "I won't go!" → She refused to go.
• "I'll do it tomorrow." → He agreed to do it the next day.
Pattern 2: verb + object + to + infinitive
Verbs: tell, ask, advise, order, warn, remind, encourage, invite, beg, persuade
• "Study harder." → The teacher advised me to study harder.
• "Don't be late." → She warned him not to be late.
• "Remember to call your mum." → He reminded me to call my mum.
Pattern 3: verb + (that) clause
Verbs: say, admit, claim, complain, deny, explain, mention, promise, suggest
• "I broke the window." → He admitted that he had broken the window.
• "I didn't take the money." → She denied that she had taken the money.
• "It's not fair!" → He complained that it was not fair.
Pattern 4: verb + -ing (gerund)
Verbs: admit, deny, suggest, recommend, mention, regret
• "I broke the window." → He admitted breaking the window.
• "Let's go to the park." → She suggested going to the park.
• "I didn't steal it." → He denied stealing it.
Pattern 5: verb + preposition + -ing
Verbs: apologize for, accuse (sb) of, insist on, complain about, congratulate (sb) on
• "I'm sorry I'm late." → He apologized for being late.
• "You stole my pen!" → She accused him of stealing her pen.
• "You must pay now." → He insisted on paying immediately.

Past Perfect Simple Passive
Rules • Examples • Multiple-Choice Practice
1. Quick Review — Active vs. Passive
In an ACTIVE sentence, the SUBJECT does the action.
• The chef cooked the meal.  — (subject = the chef, the doer)
In a PASSIVE sentence, the SUBJECT receives the action. We focus on WHAT happened, not WHO did it.
• The meal was cooked (by the chef).  — (subject = the meal, the receiver)
General passive formula:  Subject + form of 'be' + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)
2. What is the Past Perfect Simple Passive?
It is the passive form of the Past Perfect Simple tense. We use it to describe an action that:
happened BEFORE another past action or moment in the past, AND
was DONE TO the subject (not done by the subject).
In short: it talks about something that HAD BEEN DONE to someone or something at a point earlier in
the past.
3. The Formula
FORM:  Subject + had been + past participle (V3) + (by + agent)
Notice: 'had been' is used for ALL subjects (I, you, he, she, it, we, they). It does NOT change.
Examples:
• The letter had been written.  — (before another past event)
• The thieves had been caught by the police before midnight.
• All the cookies had been eaten by the time I arrived.
Rasha Aljameel
How to Build It from the Active Form
Active (Past Perfect Simple) Passive (Past Perfect Simple Passive)
Someone had stolen the car. The car had been stolen.
They had finished the project. The project had been finished.
The hotel had cancelled all bookings. All bookings had been cancelled (by the hotel).
Workers had repaired the road before sunrise. The road had been repaired before sunrise.
Steps to Convert Active → Passive
1.1. Move the OBJECT of the active sentence to the front (it becomes the new subject).
2.2. Use 'had been' as the auxiliary.
3.3. Change the main verb to its past participle (V3) form.
4.4. Add 'by + agent' if the doer is important; otherwise, omit it.
4. Negative and Question Forms
Negative
Subject + had NOT been + past participle
Short form: had not = hadn't
• The window had not been broken before the storm.
• The keys hadn't been found by the time we left.
Yes / No Questions
Had + subject + been + past participle ... ?
• Had the work been finished by 6 p.m.?
• Had the message been delivered before he arrived?
Wh- Questions
Wh-word + had + subject + been + past participle ... ?
• Where had the painting been hidden?
• Why had the meeting been cancelled?
Rasha Aljameel
5. When Do We Use It?
Use 1 — An action completed BEFORE another past action
• By the time we arrived, the cake had been eaten.  — (eating happened before our arrival)
• When she got home, the door had already been locked.
Use 2 — Reported speech (backshift from present perfect passive)
• Direct: "The thief has been arrested."
• Reported: He said the thief had been arrested.
Use 3 — When the doer is unknown, unimportant, or obvious
• The temple had been built thousands of years before.  — (we don't know exactly who built it)
• The criminal had been sentenced to ten years.
Use 4 — In formal, written, or news contexts
• By Monday, the report had been submitted to the committee.
6. Common Time Expressions
These signal words often appear with the Past Perfect Passive:
by + (a past time):  by 6 p.m., by 1990, by Friday
by the time + (past clause):  by the time he arrived...
before + (a past action):  before the guests came
already, just, never, yet
when + (past action):  when we got there...
• By 8 p.m., the report had been completed.
• The room had already been cleaned when she got home.
• Nothing had been done before the deadline.
7. Common Mistakes to Avoid
✘ The car has been stolen yesterday. → ✔ The car had been stolen before I noticed.
✘ The work was been finished. → ✔ The work had been finished.
✘ The cake had been eat. → ✔ The cake had been eaten. (use V3, not V1)
✘ Had been the door locked? → ✔ Had the door been locked?
✘ The book had been wrote by Dickens. → ✔ The book had been written by Dickens.
⚠
 Do not confuse with other passives:
Rasha Aljameel
Tense Passive Form Example
Past Simple was / were + V3 The letter was sent.
Present Perfect has / have been + V3 The letter has been sent.
Past Perfect had been + V3 The letter had been sent.
Past Continuous was / were being + V3 The letter was being sent.

The Third Conditional
Rules • Examples • Multiple-Choice Practice
1. Quick Review — Types of Conditionals
A conditional sentence has TWO parts:
the IF-clause (the condition):  If I have time...
the MAIN clause (the result):  ...I will call you.
There are four main types of conditionals in English:
Type Use Example
Zero general truths / scientific facts If you heat ice, it melts.
First real / possible future situations If it rains, I will stay home.
Second unreal / imaginary present situations If I were rich, I would travel the world.
Third unreal past — things that did NOT
happen If I had studied, I would have passed.
2. What is the Third Conditional?
The Third Conditional is used to talk about UNREAL situations in the PAST — situations that did NOT
happen but we IMAGINE what would have been different IF they had happened.
It often expresses:
Regret about the past
Criticism of past actions
Imagining a different past outcome
The key idea: it talks about the OPPOSITE of what really happened.
• If I had studied, I would have passed the exam.  — (Reality: I didn't study, so I didn't pass.)
• If she had taken a taxi, she wouldn't have been late.  — (Reality: she didn't take a taxi, so she was
late.)
• If we had known about the traffic, we would have left earlier.  — (Reality: we didn't know, so we
didn't leave earlier.)
Rasha Aljameel
3. The Formula
FORM:
If + Subject + had + past participle (V3),  Subject + would have + past participle (V3)
In simple terms:
IF-clause:  If + past perfect (had + V3)
MAIN clause:  would have + past participle (V3)
If-Clause (the condition) Main Clause (the result)
If + had + past participle would have + past participle
If I had known... ...I would have helped you.
If they had left earlier... ...they would have caught the train.
If she had asked me... ...I would have given her the answer.
Word Order — Two Possibilities
You can put the IF-clause FIRST or SECOND:
Order 1: If-clause + comma + main clause
• If I had seen him, I would have said hello.
Order 2: Main clause + (no comma) + if-clause
• I would have said hello if I had seen him.
⚠
 Use a comma ONLY when the if-clause comes first.
Rasha Aljameel
4. Negative Forms
You can make EITHER clause negative — or both — depending on what really happened.
Sentence Reality
If I had studied, I would have passed. I didn't study; I didn't pass.
If I had not (hadn't) been tired, I would have come. I was tired; I didn't come.
If she had called, I would not (wouldn't) have left. She didn't call; I left.
If he hadn't lied, she wouldn't have been angry. He lied; she was angry.
Short Forms
had not = hadn't
would not have = wouldn't have
'd have = would have  (e.g., I'd have helped = I would have helped)
'd = had  in the if-clause  (If I'd known = If I had known)
5. Variations with Modal Verbs
In the main clause, instead of 'would have', we can use OTHER modals to change the meaning slightly:
Modal Meaning Example
would have + V3 certain result If I had studied, I would have
passed.
could have + V3 ability / possibility (was able to) If I had had time, I could have
helped you.
might have + V3 weak possibility If we had left earlier, we might
have caught the bus.
should have + V3 expectation / advice (regret) If you had asked, I should have
explained it.
Rasha Aljameel
6. Expressing Regret with the Third Conditional
A very common use of the Third Conditional is to express REGRET about the past. We can also use 'I
wish' or 'If only' for the same idea.
• If I had taken your advice, I wouldn't be in this mess.
• If I had saved more money, I could have bought that car.
• If she hadn't shouted at him, he wouldn't have left.
Compare with 'I wish' / 'If only' + past perfect:
• I wish I had studied harder.  — (but I didn't)
• If only I had listened to my mother!  — (but I didn't)
7. Common Mistakes to Avoid
✘ If I would have known, I would have come.  →  ✔ If I HAD known, I would have come.
⚠
 Never use 'would have' in the if-clause.
✘ If I had studied, I would passed.  →  ✔ If I had studied, I would HAVE passed.
⚠
 Don't forget the word 'have' after 'would' in the main clause.
✘ If she had came, I would have been happy.  →  ✔ If she had COME, I would have been happy.
⚠
 Use the past participle (V3), not the past simple (V2), after 'had'.
✘ If I had knew, I would have called.  →  ✔ If I had KNOWN, I would have called.
✘ If I had studied I would have passed.  →  ✔ If I had studied, I would have passed.
⚠
 Use a comma when the if-clause comes first.
Rasha Aljameel

1. Introduction — The Three Verb Forms
In English, when one verb is followed by ANOTHER verb, the second verb takes one of three forms:
to + infinitive  →  to go, to eat, to study
infinitive (bare / base form)  →  go, eat, study  (no 'to')-ing form (gerund)  →  going, eating, studying
The form depends on the FIRST verb. There is no single rule, so we need to LEARN which verbs take
which form.
• She wants TO LEAVE early.  — (want + to + infinitive)
• She made him LEAVE early.  — (make + bare infinitive)
• She enjoys LEAVING early.  — (enjoy + -ing)
2. Verb + to-infinitive
FORM:  Subject + verb + to + base verb
This pattern is often used after verbs that express:
future plans, decisions, intentions, hopes, refusals
verbs of asking, agreeing, deciding, promising

want I want to learn French.
decide They decided to move abroad.
hope She hopes to win the prize.
plan We plan to travel next month.
promise He promised to call me back.

refuse They refused to leave the room.

Common Verbs Followed by 'to-infinitive'
Rasha Aljameel
Verb + Object + to-infinitive
Some verbs use a pattern with an OBJECT (a person) before the to-infinitive:
Pattern:  Verb + object + to + base verb
Common verbs: tell, ask, advise, allow, want, expect, invite, encourage, force, remind, warn, persuade,
teach
• She told ME to wait.
• They invited US to join the party.
• My teacher encouraged ME to apply.
3. Verb + Bare Infinitive (without 'to')
FORM:  Subject + verb + (object) + base verb  (NO 'to')
This is a special pattern used after specific verbs — mostly modals, causative verbs, and verbs of
perception.
Group 1 — Modal Verbs
All modal verbs are followed by the bare infinitive (no 'to').
Modals: can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would
• You must STUDY harder.  — (NOT: must to study)
• She can SPEAK three languages.
• They should LEAVE now.
Group 2 — Causative Verbs: make, let, have
Pattern:  make / let / have + object + base verb
• The teacher made us REPEAT the sentence.  — (NOT: made us to repeat)
• My parents let me STAY out late.
• I had the mechanic FIX my car.
⚠
 Note: 'help' can take EITHER form — both are correct:
• She helped me CARRY the bag.  — (common in spoken English)
• She helped me TO CARRY the bag.  — (also correct)
Group 3 — Verbs of Perception (see, hear, watch, feel, notice)
When we see/hear a COMPLETE action from start to finish, we use the bare infinitive.
Pattern:  see / hear / watch + object + base verb
• I saw him CROSS the road.  — (I saw the whole action)
• We heard her SING a beautiful song.
• She watched the children PLAY in the park.
⚠
 Compare with -ing form, which describes an action IN PROGRESS:
• I saw him CROSSING the road.  — (action was in progress when I saw it)
Important: Passive Form Changes!
When 'make' or verbs of perception are used in the PASSIVE, they take 'to-infinitive' (NOT bare
infinitive):
• Active: They made him APOLOGIZE.
• Passive: He was made TO APOLOGIZE.
• Active: I saw him LEAVE.
• Passive: He was seen TO LEAVE.
Rasha Aljameel
4. Verb + -ing (Gerund)
FORM:  Subject + verb + -ing form
The -ing form is often used after verbs expressing:
likes / dislikes / preferences

The Silk Road: A Global Route
Cross-Cultural Evidence
A  In the ruins of the ancient Roman city of Pompeii, which was destroyed by a volcano in the year 79
CE, a mirror was found. The mirror was from India. In the tomb of Li Xian, a Chinese military official
who died in 569 CE, archaeologists found a water pitcher that combined different styles: the shape was
from Persia (today's Iran), many details were from Central Asia, and the figures on the side were Greek
stories from the Trojan War. In the Japanese city of Nara, the 8th-century Shosoin Treasure House holds
thousands of beautiful objects — furniture, musical instruments, weapons, fabric, and military armor —
that came from regions as distant as Vietnam, western China, Iraq, the Roman Empire, and Egypt.
Clearly, long before the globalization of our modern world, trade was already going on between very
distant lands, and these objects tell a story about a place and a time.
What was the Silk Road?
B  From ancient times, cultures have influenced each other along the famous Silk Road. It was not truly
one continuous road; rather, it was a 5,000-mile network of trails that connected East Asia to the
Mediterranean. In ancient times, it was never actually called the "Silk Road." The term itself was coined
in the 19th century by a German explorer who was thinking of silk fabric from China — one of the
goods that people in the West desired most. For centuries, the Chinese kept the way silk was produced a
closely guarded secret, and they exchanged this fabric for Mediterranean glass, whose production was, in
turn, kept secret by the Romans. However, merchants moved many other goods along these routes too:
spices such as cinnamon, musical instruments, tea, valuable stones, wool, linen, and other fabrics. Ideas
and knowledge also moved along the Silk Road; travellers to foreign regions took with them ideas about
art, architecture, styles of living, and religion.
C  In a sense, there were two Silk Roads — the literal, historical one and the figurative one. The
historical network of trails was used from approximately 100 BCE until the 16th century CE. Almost
nobody actually made a complete trip from one end to the other. Instead, merchants used to carry goods
along one section of the road and sell them to other merchants at an oasis in the desert or a town in the
mountains. These merchants, in turn, took the goods to the next stop, and so on. The figurative Silk
Road, on the other hand, is a symbol of the exchange of knowledge across different cultures, and this
continues even today. In short, the Silk Road was the way that goods and ideas moved across a vast area
of Asia and southeastern Europe.
Art, Religion, and the Silk Road
D  Art and architecture reflect the movement of religion from region to region. At various times,
Buddhism, Zoroastrianism, Christianity, and Islam, among other religions, spread along the Silk Road.
Buddhism and Islam were an especially significant influence. Buddhism moved north and east from
India beginning in the 4th century CE, and when it entered China, the Buddhist love of painting and
statues moved with it. However, there was at least one notable change in style: the bare chest of the
figure of the Buddha in India was not considered proper by the Chinese, who created figures of the
Buddha wearing a robe instead.
E  As in many religions worldwide, caves deep inside mountains seem to have been important holy
places for Buddhists. For example, in Dunhuang, China — a desert oasis far from any large town or city
— Buddhists dug a series of caves and decorated them with beautiful frescoes (wall paintings) and
statues. In these caves, called Mogaoku in Chinese, the frescoes are not all religious; many depict scenes
of daily life. Evidence of some of the many Silk Road cultures was also discovered there: in one cave,
documents were found that had been written in five languages — Chinese, Uigur, Sogdian, Tibetan, and
Sanskrit.

Baby Talk: A Universal Language?
A  Anyone who has watched an adult speak to an infant knows how unusual it can sound. Why do
grandmothers babble, fathers pull funny faces, and complete strangers copy the noises a baby makes?
Surprisingly, this so-called "baby talk" is exactly what an infant's developing brain needs in order to
acquire language. Adults seem to know instinctively that this gentle, playful tone is the one babies link
with feeding, warmth and safety, which is why they use it without even thinking.
B  Even at the earliest stages of life, babies are able to grasp the general meaning of what is said and to
interpret emotional tones. For this reason, experts encourage parents to communicate with their child
early and often. Smiling reassures the baby and invites more cooing and gurgling. Looking directly at the
infant helps parents decode both verbal and non-verbal signals, such as gestures that may show
frustration or joy. Imitating a baby's sounds — echoing a clear "da-da" — feels like praise and
encourages repetition. Above all, talking to babies should resemble a real conversation: a back-and-forth
exchange rather than a one-way lecture.
C  Researchers such as Campbell Leaper argue that language ability is shaped by both nature and
nurture. Biology gives the brain its starting point, but the choices parents make — the toys offered, the
activities shared, the words spoken — strongly influence how that ability develops.

Cyberbullying: A Modern Challenge
A  Cyberbullying takes place when one person uses the Internet to treat another person cruelly. It can
happen through emails, instant messages, text messages, blogs, or — most commonly today — social
media platforms. As more young people gain access to smartphones and tablets, this harmful behaviour
continues to spread, and recent studies suggest that a surprisingly large number of users have already
been targeted at some point.
B  Online cruelty appears in many different forms. Some bullies are simply rude and may not even realise
that their words break the basic rules of online etiquette; the wisest response is often to ignore such
comments before they grow into a larger conflict. Far more dangerous, however, are those who
deliberately hack into another person's accounts to damage their reputation, or who post photographs and
videos of others without their permission.
C  Fortunately, there are several ways to protect yourself. Keep passwords completely private, log out
whenever you use a public device, and be careful before sharing personal images, since anything posted
online can remain there forever. Reviewing your privacy settings regularly is also essential. If you are
already being bullied, contact the website involved and report the abuse — most platforms will remove or
block such material. Talk to someone you trust, and save screenshots of the bully's messages, especially
if you may need to take legal action later.
D  Finally, do not forget that you can also help others. If you notice someone being targeted online,
support them. A few kind words can sometimes make all the difference.
